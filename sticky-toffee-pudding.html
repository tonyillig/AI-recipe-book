Sticky Toffee Pudding

Coffee-Soaked Dates · Beef Suet · Fallow-Style Toffee Sauce

✦
~90 Minutes
10–12 Servings
Med Difficulty
250g Dates

Here's the thing about Sticky Toffee Pudding: it has absolutely zero business being this good. Dates. Coffee. Beef fat. A brown sugar sauce with a splash of soy sauce. On paper, that reads like a fever dream. In your mouth, it tastes like the best dessert you've ever had in a countryside pub on a rainy Tuesday in England, which is the highest honor a dessert can achieve. This is the Fallow restaurant version, which means some unhinged but deeply intentional chef upgrades are baked in — and we improved it further, because the AI does not stop at "good enough." We stop at "you'll be thinking about this for weeks." No cap.

01 Ingredients
The Sponge — Main Characters
  • 250 g pitted Medjool dates, roughly chopped — Medjool over regular dried dates; they're fudgier, stickier, and more caramel-forward. Upgrade is non-negotiable.
  • 240 ml strong brewed coffee, hot — not boiling; it deepens the date flavor without tasting like coffee. You will not taste coffee in the final pudding. Trust.
  • 1½ tsp baking soda — goes into the hot date mixture; this is what makes the sponge light instead of dense
  • 150 g self-raising flour — or 150 g all-purpose flour + 1½ tsp baking powder if you don't have SR flour
  • ½ tsp flaky sea salt — Maldon or similar; the texture matters here, don't use fine salt
  • 150 g dark brown sugar, packed — dark, not light; more molasses, more depth, more of that sticky pudding energy
  • 3 large eggs, room temperature — take them out of the fridge 30 min before you start; cold eggs don't incorporate properly
  • 1½ tsp vanilla bean paste — paste over extract; you want those little specks in there. It's aesthetic AND delicious. That's called a double win.
  • 100 g beef suet — the original, traditional British fat for this dish. Gives a lighter, flakier crumb than butter. Find it in the baking aisle or at a butcher. ↑ upgrade
The Sponge — Secret Weapons new additions
  • 1 tsp ground cinnamon — just a whisper; it rounds out the date flavor and makes the whole sponge warmer and more complex
  • ½ tsp ground cardamom — this is the one that makes people say "what IS that?" It's subtle, it's floral, and it pairs insanely well with coffee and dates
  • 1 tbsp black treacle or blackstrap molasses — added to the egg mixture; deep, bitter-sweet, and it turns the sponge a gorgeous mahogany color before it even goes in the oven
The Toffee Sauce
  • 200 ml heavy cream — double cream if you're in the UK; the higher fat content makes the sauce silkier
  • 150 g dark brown sugar, packed
  • 50 g unsalted butter, cubed and cold — cold butter emulsifies better into the hot sauce; same principle as finishing a risotto
  • 1 tbsp light soy sauce — the Fallow restaurant move. It doesn't taste like soy sauce in the final dish. It tastes like depth. It tastes like a Michelin star decided to show up uninvited and stay forever.
  • 1 tsp vanilla bean paste — stir in at the very end, off the heat
  • pinch flaky sea salt — finish the sauce with this; salted toffee sauce is a different category of experience entirely

  • 19×13 inch (23×33 cm) baking dish, well buttered — or use individual ramekins for a plated restaurant vibe (see tip below)
  • 1 largeheatproof bowl — for the date soak

03 The Sponge (No Skipping, frl)
1

Soak the Dates in Hot Coffee

In your large heatproof bowl, combine the 250 g of chopped Medjool dates with 240 ml of hot (not boiling) strong brewed coffee. Let it sit for about 2 minutes, then stir in the 1½ teaspoons of baking soda. The baking soda will react with the coffee and dates immediately — it'll foam up slightly and turn a deeper, darker color. That's the reaction you want. Now let the whole thing rest for 15 minutes. The dates will soften into an almost jammy, spreadable consistency. After 15 minutes, mash lightly with a fork until the mixture is thick and coarse — some texture should remain. Do not purée this. Do not use a blender. Do not make it smooth. The little chunks of date in the finished sponge are where the flavor lives and they're the reason you'll be going back for a third serving.

The hot coffee + baking soda foam-up is the most dramatic thing that happens in this recipe and it's not even the main event. Bestie the date mixture is just getting ready. It's in its prep arc.
2

Mix the Dry Ingredients

In a medium bowl, whisk together the 150 g of self-raising flour, ½ teaspoon of flaky sea salt, 1 teaspoon of cinnamon, and ½ teaspoon of cardamom. The spices go with the flour, not the wet mix — distributing them through the dry ingredients means they'll be evenly dispersed throughout the sponge rather than clumped. Set aside. This took you 45 seconds. You're ahead of schedule.

The cardamom and cinnamon are new additions to the base recipe and they are the reason this version is different. They're subtle — no one will say "this tastes like cinnamon." They'll just say it tastes somehow warmer and more complex than any sticky toffee pudding they've had before. That mystery is yours to keep.
3

Build the Wet Mix

In your large mixing bowl, whisk together the 150 g dark brown sugar, 3 large room-temperature eggs, 1½ teaspoons of vanilla bean paste, and the 1 tablespoon of black treacle or molasses. Whisk for about 1 minute until smooth and slightly thickened — you want the sugar dissolved and the eggs fully incorporated. The mixture will turn a gorgeous dark caramel color from the treacle. Next, stir in the 100 g of beef suet. Just fold it in until evenly distributed — you'll see little white flecks throughout, and that is exactly right. Suet doesn't melt into the batter the way butter does; it keeps its shape until it hits the oven heat, which is what gives this sponge its characteristic light, almost flaky crumb. It's a different texture than butter-based puddings and it is categorically better.

Adding beef suet to a dessert is the most unhinged thing this recipe asks you to do and it is also the most correct thing. Suet was the original fat for this dish. Butter is a newcomer. We are honoring tradition and thriving.
4

Combine Everything — Gently

Add the soaked date mixture (all of it, including every drop of liquid) directly into the wet egg and sugar bowl. Stir to combine. Then gently fold in the flour and spice mixture with a spatula until just combined — stop the moment you can't see dry flour anymore. The batter will look very loose, almost pourable, and slightly glossy. That is correct. You might second-guess yourself. You might think "this seems thin, should I add more flour?" The answer is no. Overmixing develops gluten and makes the sponge dense and chewy instead of light and tender. Put the spoon down and walk away proud.

Folding means gentle, sweeping motions from the bottom up. Not stirring. Not whisking. The batter should look slightly underdone when it goes in the pan — that's the point. Trust the process. Trust the dates.
5

Bake It

Preheat your oven to 350°F (175°C). Pour the batter into your well-buttered 9×13 inch dish and spread it out evenly. Bake for 35–45 minutes. You'll know it's done when the top is set and no longer jiggles, the center springs back lightly when you press it with a finger, and a skewer inserted into the center comes out moist but not raw — sticky from the dates is fine, raw batter is not. The top should be a deep mahogany brown (that's the treacle and dark sugar doing their thing) and the edges will have pulled away slightly from the sides. Remove from the oven and let it rest in the pan for 5–10 minutes before you pour any sauce on it. Resting lets the structure set so the sauce absorbs in rather than just pooling.

Every oven runs differently. Start checking at 33 minutes, especially if your oven runs hot. If the top browns too fast before the center is done, tent it loosely with foil for the remaining time.
While the Pudding Bakes
04 The Toffee Sauce
1

Melt the Sugar into the Cream

In your medium saucepan, combine the 200 ml of heavy cream and 150 g of dark brown sugar. Set over medium heat and stir frequently until the sugar is fully dissolved and the mixture is gently bubbling — about 4–5 minutes. Don't rush this with high heat or the sugar will seize. Patience is the ingredient that separates a smooth, glossy sauce from a grainy, broken one.

This is the part of the recipe where your kitchen starts smelling like a caramel factory and you will immediately feel like a professional chef. Just let it happen. You've earned it.
2

Build the Gloss — Butter and Soy

Whisk in the 50 g of cold cubed butter a few pieces at a time. Cold butter emulsifies into the hot sauce and creates that glossy, thick, luxurious texture. Once the butter is fully incorporated, add the 1 tablespoon of soy sauce. Simmer gently for 5–7 more minutes, stirring occasionally, until the sauce thickens enough to coat the back of a spoon. When you drag your finger across the spoon, the line should hold. Remove from heat. Stir in the 1 teaspoon of vanilla bean paste and the pinch of flaky sea salt. Taste it. It should be sweet, complex, slightly salty, and deeply caramelized. The soy sauce won't be identifiable — it's just depth. It's what makes people say "this tastes like something from a real restaurant." Because now it does.

The sauce can be made up to 3 days ahead and stored in the fridge. Reheat gently in a saucepan over low heat, stirring. It thickens more as it cools, so if it seems too thick when reheating, just add a splash of cream and stir.
One tablespoon of soy sauce in a toffee dessert sauce. This is the most chaotic correct thing in modern British cooking. The Fallow chefs said "what if we just—" and the rest is culinary history. We honor it. We respect it. We also put vanilla and flaky salt in there because we're not stopping at good.
The Grand Finale
05 Assembly — This Part is Critical
1

Poke, Pour, and Rest

While the pudding is still warm (not hot — 5–10 minutes out of the oven is the sweet spot), use a skewer or chopstick to poke holes all over the surface. About 20–30 holes, evenly distributed. These channels are the sauce's entry points. Pour one-third of the hot toffee sauce over the pudding and let it seep into the holes and down the sides. Let it rest for 10–15 minutes. During this rest the sauce will soak into the sponge, making the interior sticky, gooey, and deeply saturated with toffee flavor in a way that just pouring sauce on top could never achieve. This is the step that makes people's eyes close when they take the first bite. Do not skip it. Do not rush it.

If you pour all the sauce over before serving, you lose the experience of hot sauce on warm pudding. Keep ⅔ of the sauce back and pour it at the table. The presentation of sauce going over pudding in real time is part of the meal now.
2

Serve Immediately with Hot Sauce and Cold Cream

Cut or scoop generous portions of the sauced pudding into warm bowls or onto plates. Pour the remaining hot toffee sauce over each portion at the table — this is theater, this is the moment, let the people watch the sauce go on. Then add your cream element of choice. Serve immediately. This dessert does not wait and it does not reheat the same way — it's at its absolute peak right now, in this moment. Be in the moment. Eat the pudding.

The hot toffee sauce hitting cold clotted cream and melting it slightly while steam rises off the warm sponge — this is a cinematic moment. If no one films it for Instagram you are doing it wrong. This is content. This is dessert. These are not different things.
06 Serve With
Most Authentic
Clotted cream. The richness cuts through the sweetness and the cold-on-hot contrast is non-negotiable British pub behavior.
Modern Balance
Crème fraîche. The slight tang balances the sweetness and makes the dish feel a little more refined and a little less "I just ate an entire dessert."
Most Traditional
Warm vanilla custard poured generously over the top. A second sauce situation. Controversial? Yes. Delicious? Absolutely non-negotiable.
Storage
Sponge keeps 3 days at room temp, covered. Sauce keeps 5 days refrigerated. Reheat both gently. Feeds 10–12 — actually possibly better on day two once fully soaked.
The baked sponge (without sauce) freezes beautifully for up to 2 months. Wrap individual portions tightly. Defrost, warm in the oven at 325°F for 10 minutes, then sauce and serve. Future you will send present you a thank-you note.
350°F Oven Temp 175°C. Don't go higher or the outside overcooks before the center sets.
35–45 Minutes Check at 33 min. Skewer should come out sticky, not wet.
22–26 Ramekin Time For individual portions. Check earlier — smaller volume cooks faster.
