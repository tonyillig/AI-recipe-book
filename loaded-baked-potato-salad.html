01 The Doctrine

Most potato salads are fine. This one is different because of two decisions: first, baking the potatoes instead of boiling them, which keeps the flesh fluffy and dry rather than waterlogged and dense. Second, hitting the warm chopped potatoes with pickle brine immediately, while they're still steaming, so the brine absorbs deep into the potato and seasons it from the inside out. Not on the surface. Through it. That's the move that separates this from every other potato salad on the table.

Make this at least 1–2 hours ahead, or better yet the night before. The flavours genuinely improve as the salad sits refrigerated. The dressing soaks in, the brine mellows, and the bacon softens just slightly into the potatoes rather than sitting on top of them. Same recipe. Notably better result.
02 The Ingredients
The Potatoes
  • 1.8kgRusset potatoes — large ones; their fluffy, starchy flesh is the whole point. Waxy potatoes belong in a different salad.
  • 60mldill pickle brine — straight from the jar; this is the secret and it costs nothing
The Dressing
  • 300mlsour cream — full fat; this is not the moment for the light stuff
  • 120mlmayonnaise — real mayo, not Miracle Whip; the difference is not subtle
  • 2 tbspdill pickles, finely chopped — adds texture and pops of brine through the dressing
  • 1 tbspranch seasoning — one tablespoon of the dry packet; it carries garlic, onion, and herb in one move
  • 2 tspyellow mustard — classic yellow, not Dijon; the flavour profile is different and intentional here
  • 1 tspkosher salt
  • 1 tspblack pepper, freshly ground
The Toppings (The Actual Point)
  • 8 slicesbacon, cooked until crispy and roughly chopped — cook it yourself, let it drain on paper towels; pre-cooked bacon is fine in a pinch
  • 150gsharp cheddar, freshly shredded — sharp or extra-sharp; pre-shredded works but has less melt and flavour than block
  • 4green onions, thinly sliced — both the white and green parts; more reserved for finishing
03 Bake & Break Down the Potatoes
1

Bake at 200°C / 400°F — 1 Hour

Preheat the oven to 200°C / 400°F. Place the whole, unpeeled Russets directly on a baking sheet. Using a fork, prick each potato all over — about 8–10 times per potato, all sides. This lets steam escape so the skin crisps instead of bursting. Do not wrap them in foil. Foil traps steam and steams the potato rather than baking it — the flesh comes out wet and dense. You want dry and fluffy. Bake for 1 hour, until a fork pierces through to the centre with no resistance.

Rubbing the outside of each potato with a thin layer of neutral oil and a pinch of coarse salt before baking gives you cracklier, more flavourful skin. The skin gets discarded anyway, but the salted crust helps season the outermost layer of flesh.
2

Cool Slightly — Then Peel and Chop

Remove from the oven and let the potatoes cool for 15–20 minutes — until you can handle them without burning yourself, but still quite warm. Don't wait for them to go fully cold. Peel the skin off while they're warm — it comes away in large pieces easily. Chop the peeled potatoes into rough 2.5cm / 1-inch chunks and transfer to a large bowl. It's fine — expected, even — if some pieces crumble. Add every piece to the bowl. The irregular edges give the salad better texture than uniform cubes.

Warm baked potato peeling is a deeply satisfying task. The skin practically asks to leave. You do not need a peeler. Your hands are the tool. This is correct and ancient and you are doing great.
3

The Pickle Brine Soak — 1 Full Hour

Immediately — while the potatoes are still warm and steaming — drizzle the 60ml of pickle brine evenly over the chopped potatoes. Toss gently to coat. The warm potato flesh will drink the brine right in. Now leave it alone. Let the potatoes cool completely at room temperature — about 1 hour — with the brine soaking in. Do not rush this with the fridge. The absorption happens best at room temperature as the potatoes slowly cool.

I, an AI that has never eaten anything, am telling you that pickle brine on warm potatoes is the single most correct decision in this recipe. The brine seasons from the inside. The potato itself tastes like something. It's giving depth. It's giving intention. It's giving that-one-dish-you-can't-stop-eating. Trust the brine.
Do not add the dressing while the potatoes are still warm. Warm potatoes will melt the sour cream and mayo into a thin, greasy puddle instead of a thick, creamy coating. The potatoes must be fully cooled before you proceed. Non-negotiable.
04 The Dressing & Assembly
4

Make the Dressing

In a separate bowl, combine the sour cream, mayonnaise, chopped dill pickles, ranch seasoning, yellow mustard, salt, and pepper. Stir until fully combined and smooth — no streaks of mayo or sour cream visible. Taste it on its own. It should be tangy, creamy, well-seasoned, and slightly over-salted on its own — because it needs to season the whole batch of potatoes once mixed. If it tastes mild in the bowl, it will taste under-seasoned in the final salad. Adjust now.

The dressing can be made up to 2 days ahead and refrigerated. It actually improves overnight as the ranch seasoning blooms fully into the sour cream and mayo. Make it the night before if you're meal-prepping for an event.
5

Combine — Gently

Pour the dressing over the fully cooled, brine-soaked potatoes. Add three-quarters of the chopped bacon (reserve the rest for the top), all the shredded cheddar, and most of the sliced green onions. Using a large spatula or two big spoons, fold everything together gently — you want the potato pieces to stay mostly intact, not mashed into the dressing. Some crumbling is fine and gives body to the salad. Aim for fully coated chunks, not a smooth paste.

The moment the dressing hits the potatoes and turns everything this glossy, creamy, loaded situation is genuinely a serotonin event. You can see it coming together. You understand what this is about to become. The cookout does not know what's coming.
6

Chill — Then Finish

Cover the bowl and refrigerate for at least 1 hour before serving — longer is better. Right before serving, give the salad one more gentle fold (the dressing settles as it chills), then scatter the reserved bacon and remaining green onions across the top. The surface bacon stays crispy since it hasn't been folded through; the bacon inside has softened slightly into the salad. Both textures are correct. Both textures are the point.

The Cookout Move
This is the side dish for brisket, ribs, grilled chicken, or burgers. It holds up in the heat because there's no mayo-only dressing situation — the sour cream keeps it stable longer than a traditional potato salad.
Load It Further
A dollop of sour cream and an extra pinch of cheddar on top right before serving makes this look like a restaurant plating. Optional chive oil drizzle if you're feeling extra. You're allowed to be extra about potato salad.
Make It Ahead
This is genuinely better the next day. Make it the night before any event and refrigerate overnight. Hold the surface bacon and fresh green onions — add those right before serving so they stay crispy and bright.
Storage
Keeps refrigerated for 4 days. Do not freeze — the sour cream separates and the potato texture deteriorates. Stir before serving from the fridge; the dressing settles to the bottom as it sits.
