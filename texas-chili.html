The Foundation
The Chili Paste
⏱ 45 min — the soul of the whole dish

Traditional Texas chili isn't made with chili powder from a jar. It's made by toasting whole dried chilies, rehydrating them, and blending them into a paste. This is the step that separates a good chili from an unforgettable one. Don't skip it. Don't substitute it. The dried chilies are at any Latin grocery store and most well-stocked supermarkets.

4dried guajillo chilies, stems & seeds removedmild-medium, earthy, fruity backbone
3dried ancho chilies, stems & seeds removedrich, dark, slightly sweet-raisin
2dried pasilla chilies, stems & seeds removedoptional but deepens the complexity
480mlboiling beef stock or waterfor rehydrating the chilies
1
Toast the Dried Chilies

Heat a dry cast iron skillet or heavy pan over medium heat. Toast the dried chilies in batches, pressing them flat with a spatula for 15–20 seconds per side. You want them fragrant and slightly darkened — not burnt. Burnt chilies are bitter and will ruin the paste. As soon as they're done, transfer to a large heatproof bowl.

Open a window or run the vent fan. Toasting chilies sends capsaicin into the air. Your eyes will know if you go too hot. Keep the heat moderate.
2
Rehydrate for 20 Minutes

Pour the boiling beef stock or water over the toasted chilies. Weigh them down with a plate to keep them submerged. Let soak for 20 minutes until soft and pliable. Reserve the soaking liquid — it's deep, dark, and intensely flavored. You'll use it in the chili.

3
Blend into Paste

Transfer the rehydrated chilies to a blender. Add 120ml of the soaking liquid. Blend until completely smooth — 60–90 seconds on high. The paste should be thick, deeply colored (almost maroon), and intensely fragrant. Strain through a medium-mesh strainer if you want it silky-smooth; skip straining if you prefer texture. Set aside.

The first time you smell that chili paste you will understand on a cellular level why this dish has been fought over in Texas since 1850. It smells like the reason for everything.
Build the Chili
02
The Main Event
Meat & Everything Else
⏱ 3–4 hours low and slow
Main Ingredients
2250gbeef chuck roast, cut into 1-inch cubeschuck over ground beef — chunks give texture
30mlneutral oil (avocado or beef tallow)
2large yellow onions, roughly diced
8garlic cloves, roughly chopped
720mlgood quality beef stocklow sodium; you'll season to taste
2 tbspmasa harina (corn flour)traditional thickener; GF naturally
1 tspground cumin
1 tspdried Mexican oregano (not Italian)
1 tspsmoked paprika
to tastekosher salt and black pepper
1
Prep & Season the Beef

Cut the chuck into 1–1½ inch cubes. Pat them very dry with paper towels — dry meat = better browning. Season generously with salt, pepper, and the smoked paprika. Work in batches so you don't crowd the pan.

2
Sear the Beef in Batches — Hard

Heat your 6-quart Dutch oven over medium-high heat. Add the oil and let it get very hot. Sear the beef in a single layer, don't move it for 2–3 minutes per side. You want deep, dark, mahogany crust. Work in 3–4 batches — don't rush this by crowding the pan. Crowding drops the temperature and you get steamed grey beef instead of seared. Transfer seared beef to a bowl.

The Maillard reaction is doing its thing. The sugars and proteins in the beef are having a whole chemical transformation right now and it's going to pay dividends for the next 3 hours. Respect the sear.
3
Soften the Aromatics

In the same pot (don't wipe it), reduce heat to medium. Add the diced onions and cook for 8–10 minutes, scraping up all the browned bits from the beef. Once the onions are soft and translucent, add the garlic, cumin, and Mexican oregano. Stir for 60 seconds.

Mexican oregano (Lippia graveolens) is not Italian oregano. It's earthier, less sweet, and carries a slight citrus note. It's the traditional choice for Texas chili. Find it at any Latin market or on Amazon.
4
Add the Chili Paste & Deglaze

Pour the chili paste directly into the pot and stir it in with the aromatics. Let it cook for 2 minutes, stirring constantly — you're cooking off the raw chili flavor and developing its depth. Then add the beef stock and the reserved chili soaking liquid. Return the seared beef to the pot. Bring to a simmer.

No beans go in this pot. This is not a debate. This is Texas. The beans have their own pot on a different night. We do not negotiate on this.
5
Simmer Low & Slow for 2.5–3 Hours

Reduce to a very low simmer — barely a bubble. Cover partially (lid ajar about 1 inch). Cook for 2.5 to 3 hours, stirring every 30 minutes, until the beef is completely tender and falling apart at the edges. The chili will thicken and deepen significantly as it cooks. Check seasoning at the 2-hour mark.

The chili is even better the next day — the flavors have time to develop overnight. Make it Saturday, eat it Sunday. Game day approved.
6
Thicken with Masa Harina

In the last 20 minutes, mix the masa harina with a little cold water or stock to make a smooth slurry. Stir it into the chili. Let it cook for 15–20 minutes uncovered — it'll thicken the chili and add a subtle corn flavor that's completely traditional and deeply good. Season final to taste with salt and a pinch of cumin.

Serve It
Traditional
In a bowl with saltine crackers crumbled on top and a pickled jalapeño. Simple, correct, non-negotiable.
The Works
White onion, shredded cheddar, sour cream, sliced jalapeño, fresh cilantro. Build your own bowl. Stack accordingly.
Frito Pie
Open a bag of Fritos, pour the chili straight in, top with cheese and onion. Eat from the bag. Zero regrets.
The question of "beans or no beans" in Texas chili was settled by state law in 1977. The Texas legislature officially designated traditional chili — no beans — as the state dish of Texas. We're on the right side of history here.

Texas. In a Bowl. 🌶️

You toasted whole dried chilies. You made them into a paste from scratch. You seared beef in proper batches. You simmered it for 3 hours and thickened it with masa harina. This is how people have been making chili in Texas for over 150 years. You just joined that tradition. Serve it hot. No beans. No apologies.
