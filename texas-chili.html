Texas Chili

The vibe: You toast dried chilis until they smell like a Southwest canyon. You soak them in warm beef stock until they're soft and yielding. You blend them into a dark, complex, deeply savory sauce. Then you cook five pounds of hand-cut chuck into that sauce until the whole thing becomes something that has absolutely no business tasting this good for what it cost to make. No beans. No tomatoes. 01
The Philosophy First
Real Texas Chili Has No Beans
⏱ Read this before you do anything else
The Doctrine — This Is Non-Negotiable

Traditional Texas chili — known formally as chili con carne, literally "chili with meat" — does not contain beans. This is not a preference. This is a definition. The International Chili Society, every serious Texas pitmaster, and generations of border cooks all agree: beans are a different dish. Beans are a side. Beans are something you serve next to the chili in a separate bowl for people who want them. The chili itself is beef and dried chili peppers and nothing else is invited to the party uninvited. This recipe respects that tradition completely. If someone asks you why there are no beans, the answer is: "Because this is Texas chili." No further explanation is required.

What makes this version exceptional is the chili paste. Most shortcuts call for chili powder — a pre-ground blend that varies wildly in quality and loses its complexity over time. We're starting from whole dried chilis: guajillo for bright, fruity, tangy heat and ancho for deep, chocolatey, raisin-like sweetness and complexity. Toasted, soaked in warm beef stock, then blended into a smooth, impossibly dark sauce — this paste becomes the backbone of everything. The beef cooks in it, the fat renders into it, and after three hours, you have something that tastes like it's been developed over days.

This recipe is sized to fill a 6-quart Dutch oven. That's not an accident — a full pot insulates itself better, braises more efficiently, and produces a deeper final flavor than a half-empty pot. Don't scale it down. Make the whole batch. The leftovers are genuinely better on day two and the freezer is your friend.
The Ingredients
02
Know What You're Working With
Everything You Need
⏱ Dried chilis from a Mexican grocery if possible — the difference is real

Your Two Chilis — Know the Difference

Guajillo
Heat: Mild–Medium · 2,500–5,000 SHU
Long, smooth, deep red-brown dried chili. Bright, tangy, almost cranberry-like heat with a slightly smoky finish. This is the sharp, vivid backbone of the chili paste — it punches up and brings the fruit and fire.
Ancho
Heat: Mild · 1,000–1,500 SHU
Dried poblano. Wide, wrinkled, very dark — almost black. Deep chocolate, dried fruit, and raisin flavor with barely any heat. This is the low, resonant, complex foundation that rounds out the guajillo and adds body to the paste.
Guajillo is the high note. Ancho is the bass line. Together they're a chili paste that tastes like someone spent three years developing it. And you're just — soaking them in beef stock and blending. That ratio of effort to result is genuinely unhinged and we are here for all of it.

Phase One — Build the Chili Paste
03
The Foundation of Everything
Toast, Soak & Blend
⏱ 30–40 minutes · the most important prep of this whole recipe

The chili paste is where the flavor of this dish lives. Every hour of braising, every spoonful you serve — it all traces back to how well you build this paste. Take your time here. Toast the chilis properly. Soak them fully. Blend until smooth. Everything downstream depends on it.

1
Prep the Dried Chilis

Before anything goes near heat, prep your chilis. Remove the stems by snapping or cutting them off at the top. Tear each chili open lengthwise and shake out the seeds. Some seeds will cling to the flesh — use your fingers to brush them out. A few seeds left behind are fine; a handful will add bitterness you can't cook out. The seeds are not where the heat lives in dried chilis (that's the veins), so removing them is purely about flavor, not spice management. Once stemmed and seeded, tear each chili into 2–3 large, rough pieces. This gives you more surface area for toasting and speeds up the soak.

After handling dried chilis, wash your hands before touching your face or eyes. Dried guajillo and ancho are mild, but the oils still linger on skin and will absolutely remind you they exist if you rub your eyes twenty minutes later. Ask anyone who's learned this the hard way.
2
Toast the Chilis Until Fragrant

Set a dry skillet or cast iron pan over medium heat. No oil. Working in batches, lay the chili pieces flat in the pan and press them down with a spatula. Toast them for 30–45 seconds per side — you want them to deepen in color slightly, become pliable and fragrant, and release a warm, almost smoky, slightly chocolatey smell that fills your kitchen. When a chili is properly toasted, it will feel softer and smell incredible. When a chili is over-toasted, it smells bitter and slightly acrid. The window between perfect and burnt is about 15 seconds, so stay present and watch them. Do not walk away. Do not check your phone. The chilis are the most important thing in the kitchen right now.

Burnt dried chilis ruin the entire paste — the bitterness cooks into everything and there's no correcting it. If a chili gets dark black patches and smells harsh, pull it out and discard it rather than letting it contaminate the soak. It's one chili. Your pot of chili will survive. The bitter paste won't.
The smell when you toast a dried guajillo in a dry cast iron pan is one of the best smells in all of cooking. It smells ancient and correct and like someone in a small town in Oaxaca knows something you're just now learning. You are learning it. Right now. It smells incredible in there.
3
Soak in Warm Beef Stock — 20 Minutes

Transfer all the toasted chili pieces into a large heatproof bowl or the Dutch oven itself. Pour 3 cups of warm (not boiling) beef stock over them. The chilis should be mostly submerged — press them down if they float. The stock should be warm enough to feel hot to the touch but not vigorously boiling; think of it like a very hot bath. Let them soak for 20 minutes, pressing them down occasionally. Over this time, the chilis will rehydrate completely — they'll go from stiff, leathery pieces to soft, swollen, deeply colored flesh that yields easily when you press it. By the end of the soak, the stock will have turned a dark brick red and smell deeply of chili. Do not discard this liquid. Every drop goes into the blender. Every drop goes into the pot. It is already flavored with the best of what the chilis have to offer.

The soaking stock at the end of 20 minutes is already a flavor bomb before you've even cooked anything. It looks like something between espresso and dark red wine and it smells like the chili you're about to make. Sip a small spoonful. Take a moment. Something special is happening.
4
Blend — Raw Onion, Garlic, Spices, Everything

Transfer the soaked chilis and every drop of their soaking stock into a blender. Add the roughly chopped white onion, smashed garlic cloves, ground cumin, dried Mexican oregano, smoked paprika, and onion powder. Blend on high for a full 60–90 seconds until the paste is completely smooth — no chunks, no stringy chili skin pieces, nothing visible except a uniformly dark, deep red-brown sauce. If your blender struggles, add a splash more stock to get things moving. The finished paste should pour like a thick tomato sauce — not watery, not stiff, but fluid and smooth. Taste it now. It will be intensely concentrated and slightly bitter on its own — that's correct. It will mellow and sweeten significantly during the long braise.

Hot liquids in a blender can explode out if you fill it too full and lid it too tightly. Fill no more than ⅔ full, hold a folded kitchen towel over the lid with your hand, and start on low before going to high. If your paste is very thick, blend in two batches. Nothing ruins a batch of chili paste like wearing it.
For an even smoother paste with no trace of chili skin (some are tougher than others), strain the blended paste through a fine mesh strainer after blending, pressing with a spatula to push everything through. This is optional but gives you a silkier final texture. Worth it for a dinner party; skip it for a weeknight batch.
Phase Two — Brown the Beef
04
The Part Most People Rush
Searing the Chuck
⏱ 25–30 minutes · done in batches · do not rush this

Chuck roast cut into 1-inch cubes is the right call for this chili. It has the fat content and connective tissue to survive three hours of braising and come out tender rather than chalky. But before it braises, it needs to sear — deeply, on all sides, in batches. Searing is Maillard reaction: sugars and proteins caramelizing on the surface into hundreds of new flavor compounds that only exist when meat hits a ripping-hot pan. You cannot get this flavor from slow braising alone. The sear is not optional.

1
Season and Dry the Beef

Pat the beef cubes completely dry with paper towels — all sides. Dry beef sears. Wet beef steams. This is the same principle as the scallops — moisture on the surface prevents contact between the meat and the pan, which kills the Maillard reaction before it starts. Season generously with 2 teaspoons of kosher salt and 1 teaspoon of black pepper, tossing to coat evenly. Let the seasoned beef sit uncovered for 10–15 minutes while you heat the pot. Room temperature beef sears better than cold beef — the cold interior stops the surface temperature from building properly.

2
Sear in Batches — Do Not Crowd the Pot

Heat your 6-quart Dutch oven over medium-high heat for 2 full minutes. Add 1 tablespoon of oil or tallow and let it shimmer. Add the first batch of beef — no more than what fits in a single layer with space between each piece. On a 6-quart Dutch oven, that's roughly one-third of your 5 pounds at a time. You'll do 3 batches. Let the beef cook without touching it for 2–3 minutes. Resist every urge to move it. When it releases naturally from the pan with a deep brown crust, it's ready to flip. Sear on 2–3 sides total — you don't need every side, just enough to build color. Transfer each batch to a plate and sear the next, adding oil as needed between batches.

Crowding the pot drops the temperature dramatically, and the beef will steam in its own moisture instead of searing. You'll end up with grey, wet-looking meat with no crust and no flavor development. Patience here pays off for every single bite of chili you eat at the end. Three batches. Twenty-five minutes. It's worth it.
The sizzle when properly dried beef hits a properly hot Dutch oven is the most correct sound in all of cooking. If it doesn't sizzle loudly when it hits the pan, the pan isn't hot enough yet. Wait for the right moment. The sound tells you everything.
3
Deglaze the Fond

After all the beef is seared and resting on a plate, there will be a beautiful layer of dark brown stuck-on bits coating the bottom of the pot. That is called the fond and it is pure concentrated flavor. Pour ½ cup of beef stock into the hot pot and scrape the bottom aggressively with a wooden spoon as it sizzles. Every brown bit that lifts off the bottom goes into the chili. Let it reduce for about 60 seconds, scraping the whole time, until the stock is slightly reduced and the fond is fully incorporated. This one step adds more depth of flavor to your finished chili than almost anything else.

The fond dissolving into the stock while you scrape the pot is the concentrated memory of every piece of beef that touched that surface. You're recovering flavor that would otherwise just get washed down the drain. It's the most efficient thing you'll do all day. The pot remembers. Use what the pot remembers.
Phase Three — Build and Braise
05
Where Everything Comes Together
The Long Braise
⏱ 2.5–3 hours low and slow · stir occasionally · the pot does the work
First 30 Minutes Uncovered. Medium-low. Paste cooks into the beef and reduces slightly.
Hour 1–2 Covered Low heat. Gentle simmer. Connective tissue begins breaking down. Don't rush.
Final 30 Uncovered Lid off. Reduce and concentrate. Adjust seasoning. Finish with vinegar.
1
Add the Paste and Beef Back to the Pot

Return all the seared beef (and any juices that collected on the plate) to the Dutch oven. Pour the entire blended chili paste over the beef. Add 2 more cups of beef stock, the 1 teaspoon of fish sauce if using, and stir everything together. The liquid should come about two-thirds of the way up the beef — not covering it completely, since the beef will release a significant amount of liquid as it braises. Bring the pot to a gentle simmer over medium heat, stirring occasionally, until the paste and stock are fully combined and the pot is bubbling steadily. Taste the braising liquid now and adjust salt — it should taste well-seasoned, slightly spicy, and deeply savory. It will concentrate during the cook, so don't over-salt at this stage.

2
First 30 Minutes — Uncovered, Medium-Low

Leave the pot uncovered for the first 30 minutes at medium-low heat. You want a gentle, steady simmer — not a rolling boil, not a completely still surface. Small bubbles should be breaking the surface consistently. Stir every 8–10 minutes, making sure to scrape the bottom of the pot so nothing sticks. During this phase, the chili paste cooks into the beef, the rawness of the blended onion and garlic cooks out, and the liquid begins to reduce slightly and darken. The color will deepen from a bright brick red to a deeper, more complex mahogany. This is correct. This is progress.

3
Cover and Braise Low — 1.5 to 2 Hours

After the first uncovered 30 minutes, put the lid on the Dutch oven and reduce heat to low. The pot should be at a very gentle simmer — the kind where you can see steam escaping around the lid but the liquid isn't aggressively bubbling. Cook for 1.5 to 2 hours, stirring every 20–30 minutes and checking the liquid level. If it's reducing below the halfway mark on the beef, add another half cup of warm stock. During this phase, the connective tissue in the chuck (collagen) slowly converts to gelatin, giving the finished chili that unctuous, thick, almost silky body. You can't rush collagen conversion — it happens at specific temperatures over specific time. Low and slow is not a suggestion. It is the chemistry.

After about 90 minutes of braising, start checking the beef. Press a piece against the side of the pot with a spoon — if it yields and shreds slightly with minimal resistance, you're getting close. If it's still firm and springy, give it another 30 minutes and check again. Target texture: tender enough to shred easily with a fork but still holding its shape as individual pieces, not completely falling apart.
You started with dried chili pods and a raw chuck roast and right now your kitchen smells like the best thing that has ever been cooked in it. The whole house smells like this. Anyone in a 200-foot radius is aware that something significant is happening. This is the slow-build arc of the recipe. You're at the climax. The pot is doing its whole thing right now.
4
Final 30 Minutes — Lid Off, Reduce and Concentrate

Once the beef is tender, remove the lid and raise the heat slightly to medium-low. You're concentrating the braising liquid into a thicker, more intense sauce for the final 30 minutes. Stir more frequently now — every 5 minutes — as the thickening liquid is more prone to sticking. The chili should thicken noticeably during this phase, coating the back of a spoon. If it gets too thick before time is up, add a splash of stock. In the last 5 minutes, stir in the 1 tablespoon of apple cider vinegar. The vinegar brightens everything, balances the richness of the fat and the depth of the chili paste, and makes the whole pot taste somehow more of itself. Taste one final time and adjust salt. This is your last chance. Season it correctly.

The thickened chili at this stage will stick to the bottom of the pot faster than you think. Stir it all the way down to the bottom every few minutes. A scorched bottom at the end of a three-hour cook is a genuinely tragic outcome that doesn't have to happen to you.
Serve It, Store It, Eat It Again Tomorrow
06
The Payoff
Serving & Storing
⏱ Day two is arguably better. Plan accordingly.

Texas chili is one of the few dishes that improves significantly overnight. The dried chili paste mellows and deepens, the beef fully absorbs the braising liquid, and the fat redistributes in a way that makes the texture richer and more cohesive. If you can make it a day ahead and reheat gently, you should. If you can't wait — and this is completely understandable — here's how to serve it right now.

The Classic Toppings
White onion finely diced, shredded sharp cheddar, a dollop of sour cream, pickled jalapeños, and fresh cilantro. Every topping is optional. The chili is complete without any of them.
Alongside
Warm flour tortillas or cornbread on the side. Fritos if you're going fully Texan about it (Frito pie is a legitimate lifestyle choice and we fully support it).
The Frito Pie Move
Spoon chili directly into an open bag of Fritos. Add cheddar and onion. Eat with a plastic fork while standing at the counter. This is not lowbrow. This is heritage.
Storage
Refrigerates for 5 days and only gets better. Freezes beautifully for 3 months in portions. Reheat gently on the stovetop with a splash of stock to loosen. The batch size is intentional — you want leftovers.
