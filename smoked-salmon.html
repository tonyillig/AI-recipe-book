01
The Night Before
The Brown Sugar Brine
⏱ 15 min prep · 8–12 hours in the fridge

The brine is where the magic starts. Salt pulls moisture out of the fish and then — when you rinse it — the fish reabsorbs a seasoned, sweetened liquid that penetrates deep into the flesh. Brown sugar balances the salt and adds a subtle caramel sweetness that plays perfectly against the smoke.

Brine Ingredients
900gfresh salmon fillet, skin-on King, sockeye, or coho — skin-on keeps it together
130gkosher salt do NOT use table salt — too fine, too salty
110gdark brown sugar, packed dark > light for deeper molasses flavor
950mlcold water must be cold — warm water starts cooking the fish
30mlsoy sauce optional but sends the umami factor to another dimension
1 tspblack pepper, cracked
2garlic, smashed
1lemon, sliced into rounds optional — adds brightness
1
Mix the Brine

Combine the cold water, kosher salt, brown sugar, soy sauce, black pepper, smashed garlic, and lemon slices if using. Stir until the salt and sugar are fully dissolved — about 2 minutes of good stirring. Taste the brine; it should be aggressively salty and sweet. That's exactly right.

Make sure the brine is cold before adding the fish. Mix with warm water to dissolve faster, then add ice to bring it back down.
2
Check for Pin Bones

Before the fish goes in, run your fingers along the center of the fillet from head to tail. Use clean needle-nose pliers or tweezers to pull each bone out at an angle toward the head end. There are usually 10–20 of them. This takes 3 minutes and it's the difference between a great experience and your guests picking bones out of their teeth.

Running your fingers down a raw salmon fillet looking for bones is truly unhinged behavior but it's part of the craft now. You're a cook. This is your life.
3
Submerge & Refrigerate

Place the salmon skin-side up in a large zip-lock bag or glass baking dish. Pour the brine over it — the fish should be fully submerged. If it floats, weigh it down with a plate. Seal and refrigerate for 8–12 hours. Don't go longer than 12 hours — over-brining makes the texture rubbery.

Do NOT leave it in the brine for 24 hours. The fish will be inedible. The salt does its job in 8–12 hours. Respect the process.
Morning of the Smoke
02
The Secret Weapon
Building the Pellicle
⏱ 1–2 hours air-drying — do NOT skip this

A pellicle is a thin, tacky, protein-based skin that forms on the surface of the fish as it dries. It is the reason smoke adheres to your salmon instead of just floating past it. Skip this step and you'll get pale, under-smoked results. Do this step and you'll get deeply colored, perfectly smoked, absolutely elite fish.

1
Rinse, Then Pat Dry

Remove the salmon from the brine and rinse it thoroughly under cold running water for 30–60 seconds. Then pat it completely dry with paper towels — both sides, including the skin. Get it as dry as you can.

2
Air Dry on a Rack

Place the salmon skin-side down on a wire rack set over a baking sheet. Refrigerate — uncovered — for 1–2 hours. You're looking for the surface to become slightly tacky and shiny, like it has a thin glaze on it. When you touch it lightly, it should feel sticky, not wet.

If it's a cool, dry day (under 65°F / 18°C), you can air-dry it outside in the shade with a fan blowing across it — it'll pellicle in 45–60 minutes.
The pellicle looks suspicious. It looks like the salmon is doing something. It IS doing something. Trust the process. Your salmon is leveling up right now and you don't even have to do anything. This is the GOAT step.
Fire It Up
03
The Main Event
Smoking on the Traeger
⏱ 3–5 hours on the grill

Choose Your Wood Pellets

Alder
The classic Pacific Northwest choice for salmon. Light, slightly sweet, delicate smoke that doesn't overwhelm the fish.
Apple
Mild, fruity, and slightly sweet. Pairs beautifully with the brown sugar brine.
Cherry
Slightly sweeter than apple with a gorgeous dark color payoff on the finished fish.
Pecan
Richer, nuttier flavor. A bold choice that pairs well with the sweetness of the brine.
Avoid hickory, mesquite, or oak for salmon. They're too aggressive and will completely overpower the fish. Save those for brisket.
165°FStart Temp74°C. First 1–2 hours. Low and slow, maximum smoke absorption.
225°FMid Temp107°C. After 2 hours. Gently raise to finish cooking through.
63°CPull Temp140–145°F internal fish temp. This is done. Get it off now.
1
Fire Up the Traeger to 165°F / 74°C

Set your Traeger to 165°F (74°C) or the "Smoke" setting and let it preheat for 15 minutes with the lid closed. Load it with your chosen pellets — alder is the move for traditional smoked salmon.

2
Place the Salmon — Skin Down, Don't Touch It

Place the salmon skin-side down directly on the grill grates. Close the lid and do not open it for at least 1 hour. Every time you open the lid you lose smoke and heat. Resist the urge.

If you're worried about the salmon sticking, lay it on a cedar plank that's been soaked in water for 1 hour. It adds a subtle woodsy flavor and makes transfer easy.
3
After 2 Hours: Raise to 225°F / 107°C

After 2 hours at 165°F, bump the temperature up to 225°F (107°C) to finish the cook. Optionally brush the salmon with a thin glaze of 30ml maple syrup or honey mixed with 1 tsp soy sauce. Reapply once more 30 minutes later if you want extra glaze action.

That glaze hitting the hot salmon surface and caramelizing? The sizzle? The color? Bro. It's giving everything. It's giving literally everything.
4
Probe It — Pull at 63°C / 145°F Internal

After about 3–4 hours total, start checking the internal temperature. You're pulling it at 63°C / 145°F. At this temperature the salmon is fully safe, moist, and flaky. Overcooked scallops get rubbery and sad — pull it the second it hits temp.

The salmon will continue cooking slightly after you pull it off the heat. Pull it at 60–62°C and let it rest — it'll coast to 63–65°C on its own.
5
Rest for 15 Minutes Before Touching It

Transfer carefully to a cutting board or serving platter. Let it rest for 15 minutes before breaking into it. The juices redistribute as it rests. If you cut into it immediately, all that moisture runs out onto the board and you get dry salmon.

The Finish Line

You Made It
Serving & Storing
⏱ The part where people lose their minds
Classic
Flaked over cream cheese on toasted bagels with capers, red onion, and a squeeze of lemon. No notes. Perfect dish.
Fancy Dinner
Whole fillet on a wooden board with crème fraîche, dill, cucumber ribbons, and crackers. Instant dinner party centerpiece.
Next-Day Lunch
Flaked into scrambled eggs with chives and a little butter. Life-changing. This is the move the morning after.
Storage
Refrigerate wrapped tightly for up to 5 days. Freeze for up to 3 months. Reheat gently at 135°C / 275°F for 10 min.
