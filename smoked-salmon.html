Smoked Salmon

The most patient thing you'll ever cook. Worth every single hour.

The vibe: You brine it the night before, wake up, fire up the Traeger, and spend the day doing literally nothing while it slowly transforms into the most impressive thing you've ever served. This is main character behavior for your grill.

01
The Night Before
The Brown Sugar Brine
⏱ 15 min prep · 8–12 hours in the fridge

The brine is where the magic starts. Salt pulls moisture out of the fish and then — when you rinse it — the fish reabsorbs a seasoned, sweetened liquid that penetrates deep into the flesh. Brown sugar balances the salt and adds a subtle caramel sweetness that plays perfectly against the smoke. This is not optional. A brined salmon and an unbrined salmon are two completely different dishes, and one of them is significantly more bussin.

Brine Ingredients
2–3 lbs Fresh salmon fillet, skin-on King, sockeye, or coho — skin-on keeps it together on the grill
½ cup Kosher salt Do NOT use table salt — too fine, too salty
½ cup Dark brown sugar, packed Dark > light for deeper molasses flavor
4 cups Cold water Must be cold — warm water starts cooking the fish
2 tbsp Soy sauce Optional but sends the umami factor to another dimension
1 tsp Black pepper, cracked Fresh cracked only, fam
2 cloves Garlic, smashed Smash with the flat of your knife — don't mince
1 Lemon, sliced into rounds Optional — adds brightness to the brine
1
Mix the Brine

In a large bowl or container, combine the cold water, kosher salt, brown sugar, soy sauce, black pepper, smashed garlic, and lemon slices if using. Stir until the salt and sugar are fully dissolved — this takes about 2 minutes of good stirring. Taste the brine; it should be aggressively salty and sweet, almost uncomfortably so. That's exactly right. The fish won't absorb all of it, and a lot washes away when you rinse.

Make sure the brine is cold before adding the fish. You can mix it with warm water to dissolve faster, then add ice to bring it back down.
2
Check for Pin Bones

Before the fish goes in, run your fingers along the center of the fillet from head to tail. You'll feel tiny little bones poking up — those are pin bones, and they need to come out. Use clean needle-nose pliers or tweezers, grip each bone, and pull it out at an angle (slightly toward the head end). There are usually 10–20 of them in a full fillet. This takes 3 minutes and it's the difference between a great experience and your guests picking bones out of their teeth at the dinner table.

Running your fingers down a raw salmon fillet looking for bones is truly unhinged behavior but it's part of the craft now. You're a cook. This is your life.
3
Submerge & Refrigerate

Place the salmon fillet skin-side up in a large zip-lock bag or glass baking dish. Pour the brine over it — the fish should be fully submerged. If it floats, weigh it down with a plate. Seal or cover and refrigerate for 8–12 hours. Overnight is perfect. Don't go longer than 12 hours on a thick fillet or 8 hours on a thinner one — over-brining makes the texture rubbery and the flavor too salty. Set a timer.

Do NOT leave it in the brine for 24 hours. The fish will be inedible. The salt does its job in 8–12 hours. Respect the process.
Morning of the Smoke
02
The Secret Weapon
Building the Pellicle
⏱ 1–2 hours air-drying — do NOT skip this

This is the step that separates good smoked salmon from legendary smoked salmon. A pellicle is a thin, tacky, protein-based skin that forms on the surface of the fish as it dries. It looks a little weird. It feels sticky. And it is the reason smoke adheres to your salmon instead of just floating past it. Skip this step and you'll get pale, under-smoked, disappointing results. Do this step and you'll get deeply colored, perfectly smoked, absolutely elite fish.

1
Rinse, Then Pat Dry

Remove the salmon from the brine and rinse it thoroughly under cold running water for 30–60 seconds. You're washing off the excess surface salt. Then pat it completely dry with paper towels — both sides, including the skin. Get it as dry as you can.

2
Air Dry on a Rack

Place the salmon skin-side down on a wire rack set over a baking sheet. Put it in your refrigerator — uncovered — for 1–2 hours. You're looking for the surface to become slightly tacky and shiny, like it has a thin glaze on it. The flesh color will deepen slightly. When you touch it lightly, it should feel sticky, not wet.

If it's a cool, dry day (under 65°F), you can air-dry it outside in the shade with a fan blowing across it — it'll pellicle in 45–60 minutes. Don't do this in summer heat or you'll start cooking it prematurely.
The pellicle looks suspicious. It looks like the salmon is doing something. It IS doing something. Trust the process. Your salmon is leveling up right now and you don't even have to do anything. This is the GOAT step.
Fire It Up
03
The Main Event
Smoking on the Traeger
⏱ 3–5 hours on the grill

Low and slow is the law here. You're not grilling — you're smoking. The goal is gentle, patient heat and consistent smoke over several hours. The Traeger makes this incredibly easy because it maintains temperature automatically. Your job is basically to check on it occasionally and not crank the heat because you're impatient. Patience is the secret ingredient.

Choose Your Wood Pellets

Alder
The classic Pacific Northwest choice for salmon. Light, slightly sweet, delicate smoke that doesn't overwhelm the fish. The traditional pairing for a reason.
Apple
Mild, fruity, and slightly sweet. Pairs beautifully with the brown sugar brine. Great if you want a subtler smoke flavor.
Cherry
Slightly sweeter than apple with a gorgeous dark color payoff. Adds beautiful mahogany color to the finished fish.
Pecan
Richer, nuttier flavor. A bold choice that pairs well with the sweetness of the brine if you want more smoke presence.
Avoid hickory, mesquite, or oak for salmon. They're too aggressive and will completely overpower the fish. Save those for brisket.
165°F Start Temp First 1–2 hours. Low and slow, maximum smoke absorption.
225°F Mid Temp After 2 hours. Gently raise to finish cooking through.
140–145°F Pull Temp Internal fish temp. This is done. Get it off now.
1
Fire Up the Traeger to 165°F

Turn your Traeger to 165°F (or the "Smoke" setting) and let it preheat for 15 minutes with the lid closed. You want it fully up to temp and producing clean smoke before the fish goes on. Load it with your chosen pellets — alder is the move for traditional smoked salmon.

2
Place the Salmon — Skin Down, Don't Touch It

Place the salmon skin-side down directly on the grill grates. Do not use foil — the grates give you better smoke circulation and a slight crust on the bottom. Close the lid and do not open it for at least 1 hour. Every time you open the lid you lose smoke and heat. Resist the urge. Go watch TV. Text your friends about how you're smoking salmon right now.

If you're worried about the salmon sticking, lay it on a cedar plank that's been soaked in water for 1 hour. It adds a subtle woodsy flavor and makes transfer a breeze.
3
After 2 Hours: Raise to 225°F

After 2 hours at 165°F, bump the temperature up to 225°F to finish the cook. At this point you can optionally brush the salmon with a thin glaze of 2 tablespoons of maple syrup or honey mixed with 1 teaspoon of soy sauce. It will caramelize into a beautiful, sticky lacquer over the next hour. Reapply once more 30 minutes later if you want extra glaze action.

That glaze hitting the hot salmon surface and caramelizing? The sizzle? The color? Bro. It's giving everything. It's giving literally everything.
4
Probe It — Pull at 140–145°F Internal

After about 3–4 hours total, start checking the internal temperature with an instant-read thermometer. Insert it into the thickest part of the fillet. You're pulling it at 140–145°F. At 140°F the salmon is fully safe, moist, and flaky. At 160°F it starts drying out. There is no coming back from overcooked smoked salmon, so don't push it. When the probe hits 140°F, the fish is done — period.

The salmon will continue cooking slightly after you pull it off the heat (called carryover cooking). Pull it at 138–140°F and let it rest. It'll coast to 142–145°F on its own.
Total smoke time varies by thickness: thin fillets (under 1 inch) can be done in 2.5–3 hours. Thick fillets (1.5 inch+) may take 4–5 hours. Always go by internal temp, not time.
5
Rest for 15 Minutes Before Touching It

Carefully slide a large spatula or two under the salmon and transfer it to a cutting board or serving platter. Let it rest for 15 minutes before breaking into it. The juices redistribute as it rests. If you cut into it immediately, all that moisture runs out onto the board and you get dry salmon. The wait is painful but completely worth it.

The Finish Line
04
You Made It
Serving & Storing
⏱ The part where people lose their minds

Smoked salmon is elite served hot right off the Traeger, but honestly — it might be even better cold the next day. The smoke flavor deepens overnight. Here's how to do it justice:

Classic
Flaked over cream cheese on toasted bagels with capers, red onion, and a squeeze of lemon. No notes. Perfect dish.
Fancy Dinner
Whole fillet on a wooden board with crème fraîche, dill, cucumber ribbons, and crackers. Instant dinner party centerpiece.
Next-Day Lunch
Flaked into scrambled eggs with chives and a little butter. Life-changing. This is the move the morning after.
Storage
Refrigerate wrapped tightly in plastic or in an airtight container for up to 5 days. Freeze for up to 3 months. It reheats gently at 275°F for 10 min.
You spent 12+ hours on this. You brined it. You pellicled it. You smoked it low and slow with alder wood. You glazed it. You waited. This fish went through a whole character arc and came out the other side absolutely iconic. Serve it like you know what you did. Because you do.

That's a Smoke. 🔥
