01 The Brown Sugar Brine

The brine is where the magic starts. Salt pulls moisture out of the fish and then the fish reabsorbs a seasoned, sweetened liquid that penetrates deep into the flesh. Brown sugar balances the salt and adds a subtle caramel sweetness that plays perfectly against the smoke. A brined salmon and an unbrined salmon are two completely different dishes.

Brine Ingredients
  • 900gfresh salmon fillet, skin-on — King, sockeye, or coho; skin-on keeps it together on the grill
  • 120gkosher salt — do NOT use table salt
  • 120gdark brown sugar, packed
  • 960mlcold water — must be cold; warm water starts cooking the fish
  • 2 tbspsoy sauce — optional but sends the umami factor to another dimension
  • 1 tspblack pepper, cracked
  • 2 clovesgarlic, smashed
  • 1lemon, sliced into rounds — optional
1

Mix the Brine

In a large container, combine 960ml cold water, 120g kosher salt, 120g brown sugar, soy sauce, black pepper, smashed garlic, and lemon slices. Stir until the salt and sugar are fully dissolved — about 2 minutes. Taste the brine: it should be aggressively salty and sweet, almost uncomfortably so. That's exactly right — the fish won't absorb all of it, and a lot washes away when you rinse.

2

Check for Pin Bones

Before the fish goes in, run your fingers along the centre of the fillet from head to tail. You'll feel tiny bones poking up — those are pin bones. Use clean needle-nose pliers or tweezers, grip each bone, and pull it out at a slight angle toward the head end. There are usually 10–20 of them in a full fillet. This takes 3 minutes and it's the difference between a great experience and your guests picking bones out of their teeth.

3

Submerge & Refrigerate for 8–12 Hours

Place the salmon skin-side up in a large zip-lock bag or glass baking dish. Pour the brine over it — the fish should be fully submerged. Seal or cover and refrigerate for 8–12 hours. Overnight is perfect. Don't go longer than 12 hours on a thick fillet — over-brining makes the texture rubbery and the flavour too salty. Set a timer.

Do NOT leave it in the brine for 24 hours. The salt does its job in 8–12 hours. Respect the process.
02 Building the Pellicle

This is the step that separates good smoked salmon from legendary smoked salmon. A pellicle is a thin, tacky, protein-based skin that forms on the surface as the fish air-dries. It looks a little weird. It feels sticky. And it is the reason smoke adheres to your salmon instead of just floating past it.

4

Rinse, Then Pat Dry

Remove the salmon from the brine and rinse thoroughly under cold running water for 30–60 seconds — you're washing off excess surface salt. Pat completely dry with paper towels on both sides, including the skin. Get it as dry as you can.

5

Air Dry on a Rack — 1 to 2 Hours

Place the salmon skin-side down on a wire rack set over a baking sheet. Put it in your refrigerator — uncovered — for 1–2 hours. You're looking for the surface to become slightly tacky and shiny, like it has a thin glaze on it. When you touch it lightly, it should feel sticky, not wet.

If it's a cool, dry day (under 18°C / 65°F), you can air-dry it outside in the shade with a fan blowing across it — it'll pellicle in 45–60 minutes. Don't do this in summer heat.
The pellicle looks suspicious. It looks like the salmon is doing something. It IS doing something. Trust the process. Your salmon is levelling up right now and you don't have to do anything. This is the GOAT step.
03 Smoking on the Traeger
6

Fire Up the Traeger to 74°C / 165°F

Set your Traeger to 74°C / 165°F (or the "Smoke" setting) and let it preheat for 15 minutes with the lid closed. Load with alder pellets — the classic Pacific Northwest choice for salmon. Light, slightly sweet, delicate smoke that doesn't overwhelm the fish.

Avoid hickory, mesquite, or oak for salmon — they're too aggressive and will completely overpower the fish. Save those for brisket.
7

Place the Salmon — Skin Down, Don't Touch It

Place the salmon skin-side down directly on the grill grates. Do not use foil — the grates give better smoke circulation. Close the lid and do not open it for at least 1 hour. Every time you open the lid you lose smoke and heat.

8

After 2 Hours: Raise to 107°C / 225°F

Once your probe reads around 38–43°C / 100–110°F internal (roughly 2 hours into the smoke at 74°C), bump the cooker temperature up to 107°C / 225°F to finish the cook. If the fish is climbing faster than expected, make the bump earlier — go by the probe, not the clock. At this point you can optionally brush the salmon with a thin glaze of 2 tablespoons of maple syrup or honey mixed with 1 teaspoon of soy sauce. It will caramelise into a beautiful, sticky lacquer. Reapply once more 30 minutes later if you want extra glaze action.

That glaze hitting the hot salmon surface and caramelising? The sizzle? The colour? It's giving everything. It's giving literally everything.
9

Probe It — Pull at 63°C / 145°F Internal

After about 3–4 hours total, start checking internal temperature with an instant-read thermometer. Insert it into the thickest part of the fillet. Pull at 63°C / 145°F. At 63°C the salmon is fully safe, moist, and flaky. At 71°C it starts drying out. There is no coming back from overcooked smoked salmon.

The salmon will continue cooking slightly after you pull it (carryover cooking). Pull at 60–62°C and let it rest — it'll coast to 63–65°C on its own.
Total smoke time varies by thickness: thin fillets (under 2.5cm) can be done in 2.5–3 hours. Thick fillets (4cm+) may take 4–5 hours. Always go by internal temperature, not time.
10

Rest for 15 Minutes Before Touching It

Carefully slide a spatula under the salmon and transfer to a cutting board or serving platter. Let it rest for 15 minutes before breaking into it. The juices redistribute as it rests. If you cut into it immediately, all that moisture runs out onto the board and you get dry salmon.

04 Serving & Storing
Classic
Flaked over cream cheese on toasted bagels with capers, red onion, and a squeeze of lemon. No notes. Perfect dish.
Fancy Dinner
Whole fillet on a wooden board with crème fraîche, dill, cucumber ribbons, and crackers. Instant dinner party centrepiece.
Next-Day Breakfast
Flaked into scrambled eggs with chives and a little butter. Life-changing. This is the move the morning after.
Storage
Refrigerate wrapped tightly for up to 5 days. Freeze for up to 3 months. Reheat gently at 135°C / 275°F for 10 minutes.
