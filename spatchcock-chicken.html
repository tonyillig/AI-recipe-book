Spatchcock Roast Chicken

Dry-Brined Overnight · Butterflied · High Heat · One Pan

Spatchcocking is the single most important technique improvement you can make to your roast chicken. By removing the backbone and pressing the bird flat, you achieve two things that a standard roast chicken cannot: even cooking on both breasts and legs simultaneously, and dramatically more skin surface exposed to direct heat — which means a much larger proportion of the bird finishes with that deep golden, crispy skin that everyone fights over. Dry-brine it overnight for seasoned, juicy meat. Roast hot and fast over a pan of aromatics. This is the weeknight hero.

01 Ingredients
  • ~1.6kgwhole chicken a good-quality free-range bird makes a real difference here
  • 2 tspkosher salt for the dry brine — increase proportionally for larger birds
  • 1 tsp eachblack pepper, garlic powder, smoked paprika
  • 30mlolive oil
  • 1 headgarlic, halved horizontally
  • 2lemons, halved
  • 6 sprigsfresh thyme
  • 2onions or shallots, roughly sliced — for the pan base
02 The Cook
1

Spatchcock the Chicken

Place the chicken breast-side down. Using sharp kitchen scissors or poultry shears, cut along each side of the backbone from tail to neck. Remove the backbone entirely (save it for stock). Flip the bird breast-side up and press down firmly on the breastbone with the heel of your hand until you feel it crack and the bird flattens. The chicken should now lie completely flat.

The backbone and any wing tips make an excellent quick stock. Cover with water, add an onion and a few peppercorns, simmer for 45 minutes. Freeze it. You'll use it.
2

Dry Brine Overnight

Mix the salt, pepper, garlic powder, and smoked paprika. Pat the chicken completely dry. Season generously on all surfaces — including under the skin wherever you can loosen it with your fingers. Rub the dry brine directly onto the flesh under the skin as well as over the top. Place uncovered on a rack over a tray in the refrigerator overnight, or for at least 4 hours. The skin will look a little dry when you take it out. That is exactly what you want.

A dry-brined chicken going into a hot oven is one of the most confident things a home cook can do. The overnight work is already done. You're just collecting the reward now.
3

Roast Hot Over Aromatics

Preheat oven to 220°C / 425°F. Scatter the sliced onions, garlic halves, lemon halves, and thyme in a roasting tray. Drizzle with olive oil. Place the spatchcocked chicken flat on top, skin-side up. Roast for 45–55 minutes, depending on size, until the skin is deeply golden and a probe thermometer in the thickest part of the thigh reads 74°C / 165°F. Rest for 10 minutes before cutting.

Do not cover the chicken during roasting — you are specifically trying to drive off surface moisture and crisp the skin. Covering it traps steam and you'll get soft skin. Soft skin is not the goal. Crispy skin is the only goal.
4

Rest and Carve

Rest the chicken uncovered for at least 10 minutes. The juices in the pan (mixed with the caramelised onions and rendered chicken fat) are an instant, incredible sauce — scrape them up and pour over the carved chicken. Cut through the leg joints and separate the thighs from the drumsticks. Slice the breasts off the bone. Serve with whatever you like.
