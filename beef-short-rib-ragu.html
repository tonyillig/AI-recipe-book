Beef Short Rib Ragù

Braised Short Ribs · San Marzano · Red Wine · Pappardelle

Here is the thing about beef short rib ragù: it asks for almost nothing from you. You sear some ribs, you build a sauce, you put a lid on it and go live your life for four hours. What comes back to you is one of the most luxurious pasta sauces in existence — braising liquid thickened with collagen, shredded beef so tender it falls apart when you look at it, a deep red wine backbone. Over pappardelle with a handful of Parmesan and a glass of whatever you used to cook with. This is the Sunday dinner that earns you points for the rest of the week.

01 Ingredients
The Ribs
  • 2.5kgbone-in beef short ribs English cut, not flanken — you want thick, meaty pieces
  • to tastekosher salt and black pepper
  • 2 tbspneutral oil
The Sauce
  • 2 largeyellow onions, diced
  • 3 mediumcarrots, diced
  • 3 stalkscelery, diced
  • 8 clovesgarlic, roughly chopped
  • 60gtomato paste
  • 750mldry red wine Barolo if you're feeling it, Chianti if you're being sensible
  • 2 cansSan Marzano whole tomatoes crushed by hand before they go in
  • 500mlbeef stock
  • 6 sprigsfresh thyme
  • 2bay leaves
To Serve
  • 500gpappardelle fresh if you can find it; dried works beautifully too
  • to finishfreshly grated Parmesan, good olive oil, fresh herbs
02 The Cook
1

Season and Sear the Ribs Hard

Pat the short ribs completely dry and season aggressively with salt and pepper on all sides. Heat a large Dutch oven over high heat with 2 tbsp oil. Sear the ribs in batches — 3–4 minutes per side — until deeply browned on all meaty surfaces. Don't crowd the pot; you want crust, not steam. Remove and set aside. You should have a beautiful layer of fond on the bottom of the pot.

The sear on short ribs is the most satisfying thing you will do in a kitchen this week. That sound. That colour. That smell. You are building flavour in real time and it's extremely obvious that you know what you're doing.
2

Build the Soffritto

Reduce heat to medium. Add the onions, carrots, and celery to the same pot and cook for 10 minutes, scraping up all the fond as you go. Add the garlic and cook another 2 minutes. Add the tomato paste and stir it into the vegetables — cook for 3 minutes until it darkens slightly and smells deeply savoury.

3

Deglaze with Wine — All of It

Pour in the entire 750ml of red wine. Let it boil and reduce by half — about 8 minutes — scraping the bottom constantly. The sauce should smell incredible at this point. Add the crushed San Marzano tomatoes, beef stock, thyme, and bay leaves. Return the short ribs to the pot. The liquid should come about two-thirds up the ribs.

Bring to a simmer on the stovetop first before covering. Starting the braise with already-hot liquid cuts 20–30 minutes off your cook time.
4

Braise Low and Slow — 3.5 to 4 Hours

Cover the Dutch oven and transfer to a 160°C / 325°F oven. Braise for 3.5 to 4 hours. The ribs are done when the meat pulls away from the bone with absolutely zero resistance and wants to fall apart. Check at 3 hours and every 30 minutes after. Every oven is different.

The oven is doing 4 hours of work right now and you are free to do literally anything else. This is the whole promise of braising. The pot just needs to be left alone to become magnificent.
5

Pull, Shred, Skim, and Reduce

Remove the ribs and let them cool slightly. Pull the meat off the bones and shred it into large pieces — discard the bones, fat cap pieces, and any cartilage. Skim the fat off the surface of the braising liquid. If the sauce seems thin, bring it to a boil on the stovetop and reduce for 10–15 minutes. Return the shredded beef to the sauce and stir to combine. Taste and season with salt.

The ragù is genuinely better made the day before. Cool completely, refrigerate overnight — the fat solidifies and can be lifted off cleanly, and the flavours deepen significantly. Reheat gently before serving.
6

Cook Pasta and Combine

Cook the pappardelle in well-salted boiling water to al dente. Reserve a full cup of pasta water before draining. Add the drained pasta directly to the ragù in the pot and toss over medium heat, adding pasta water as needed to bring everything together into a glossy, cohesive sauce. Serve in warm bowls with Parmesan, a thread of olive oil, and fresh herbs.

Never, ever rinse your pasta. The starch is what makes the sauce stick. Rinsed pasta is just sad wet noodles and the sauce will slide right off it.

Sunday Made Edible. 🍝

Short rib ragù is the dish that made slow cooking make sense to everyone who tried it. You gave it time. You gave it wine. You gave it a proper sear. What came back is something that tastes like it took all day — because it did, and that's exactly the point. Over pappardelle with Parmesan. Nothing else needed.
