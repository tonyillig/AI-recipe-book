Tomato Tart

Rough Puff Pastry · Herbed Ricotta · Heirloom Tomatoes · Summer

This tart exists because of what happens to a good tomato in a hot oven. Heirloom tomatoes, placed cut-side up on herbed ricotta, on rough puff pastry, at high heat: the tomatoes collapse, concentrate, caramelise at the edges, and release their juice into the ricotta below, which sets into something creamy and rich and deeply flavoured of tomato and herbs. The rough puff pastry — made quickly with visible flakes of cold butter — shatters and puffs and holds the whole thing. Eat warm, with the best olive oil you have, and a glass of whatever you're drinking. This is the summer dish.

01 Ingredients
The Rough Puff Pastry
  • 250gplain flour, plus extra for dusting
  • 180gcold unsalted butter, cut into 2cm cubes — keep in the freezer until needed
  • ~80mlice-cold water — added gradually
  • 1 tspsalt
The Herbed Ricotta Base
  • 250gricotta, drained if very wet
  • 40gParmesan, finely grated
  • 1 clovegarlic, minced to a paste
  • handfulfresh basil, finely chopped
  • 4 sprigsfresh thyme, leaves stripped
  • 1lemon, zested
  • to tastesalt and black pepper
The Tomatoes
  • 600gheirloom or heritage tomatoes, sliced 1cm thick a mix of colours and shapes is both beautiful and delicious
  • 3 tbspolive oil
  • flaky saltfor the tomatoes before roasting
02 The Method
1

Make the Rough Puff

Combine flour and salt in a bowl. Add the frozen butter cubes. Using your fingertips (working quickly so the butter stays cold), break the butter into irregular, flat, pea-sized pieces — not fully rubbed in. You want visible butter flakes; these create the layers. Add the ice water gradually and mix with a knife or your hands until the dough just comes together — it will look rough and shaggy. That is correct. Flatten into a rectangle, wrap in cling film, and refrigerate for 30 minutes.

The visible butter flakes in rough puff are not a mistake — they are the whole point. Those flakes create steam in the oven that puffs and separates the layers. Fully rubbed-in butter gives you shortcrust. Rough puff needs to stay rough.
2

Roll and Fold (Quick Lamination)

On a lightly floured surface, roll the chilled dough into a long rectangle. Fold the bottom third up and the top third down (like a business letter). Turn 90 degrees, roll out again, and fold again. Wrap and refrigerate for another 20 minutes. This quick two-fold process creates the layers without the full faff of proper puff pastry.

3

Make the Ricotta Base and Prep Tomatoes

Mix the ricotta, Parmesan, garlic, basil, thyme, lemon zest, salt, and pepper until combined. Taste — it should be herby, rich, and well seasoned. Slice the tomatoes, place on a tray lined with paper towels, and season with salt. Let sit for 10 minutes to draw out excess moisture. Pat dry. This step prevents a soggy-bottomed tart.

Salting the tomatoes to draw out moisture before they go on the tart is not optional extra chef behaviour. It is the difference between a crispy-bottomed tart and one that has gone soggy from tomato water. Salt them. Pat them. Proceed.
4

Assemble and Bake

Roll the chilled pastry to roughly 30×40cm. Transfer to a lined baking tray. Score a 2cm border all the way around (don't cut through). Spread the ricotta mixture inside the border. Arrange the tomato slices over the ricotta — overlapping slightly, mixed colours wherever possible. Drizzle with olive oil. Brush the border with beaten egg. Bake at 200°C / 400°F for 30–35 minutes until the pastry is deeply golden, the border has puffed, and the tomatoes are collapsed and caramelised. Rest 5 minutes, then scatter fresh basil and flaky salt. Serve warm.

The scored border is the frame that allows the pastry edges to rise while the filling stays put. Don't skip it. And don't put toppings over the border — the egg wash needs a clear surface to puff and brown properly.

Pastry. Ricotta. Tomatoes. Summer. 🍅

You made rough puff. You herbed the ricotta. You salted the tomatoes. You watched the border rise in the oven and the tomatoes collapse into the cream. This is a summer tart that requires good tomatoes — which means it has a season, which means when you make it at the right time of year it tastes extraordinary. That seasonality is part of what makes it worth making.
