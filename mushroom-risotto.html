Mushroom Risotto

Mushroom Stock · Mixed Fungi · Parmesan · Properly Stirred

Mushroom risotto done well is one of the great vegetarian main courses — deeply savoury, creamy without being heavy, and built on a mushroom stock that amplifies every layer of fungi flavour in the dish. The technique is the same as scallop risotto: keep the stock warm, add it ladle by ladle, stir consistently but not obsessively, and finish with butter and Parmesan off the heat. The difference here is the dried porcini — soaked and used to enrich both the cooking liquid and the final dish — and a proper sauté of fresh mushrooms to add texture and depth.

01 Ingredients
  • 320garborio or carnaroli rice carnaroli holds its bite better over longer cooking — use it if you can find it
  • 500gmixed fresh mushrooms — chestnut, shiitake, oyster, portobello
  • 30gdried porcini mushrooms soaked in 400ml boiling water — that liquid becomes stock
  • 1.5 litresvegetable or mushroom stock, kept warm in a separate pan
  • 200mldry white wine
  • 4 largeshallots, finely diced
  • 4 clovesgarlic, minced
  • 80gunsalted butter, cold, cubed — divided
  • 80gParmesan, finely grated
  • 6 sprigsfresh thyme
02 The Method
1

Rehydrate the Porcini and Build Your Stock

Soak the dried porcini in 400ml boiling water for 20 minutes. Lift the mushrooms out, squeeze them gently, and roughly chop. Add the soaking liquid to your stock — strain it first through a fine sieve to remove any grit. Keep the combined stock warm in a separate pan throughout the cook. Cold stock kills the risotto process.

The porcini soaking liquid is intensely flavoured mushroom stock. It is the secret weapon of this dish. Don't discard it — it goes straight into the risotto pot via the ladle.
2

Sauté the Fresh Mushrooms

Heat olive oil in a wide pan over high heat until shimmering. Add the fresh mushrooms in a single layer — in batches if needed — and cook without moving for 3–4 minutes until deeply golden. Season, toss briefly, and cook another 2 minutes. Remove and set aside. Don't skip this step — properly sautéed mushrooms add texture and flavour that mushrooms cooked in the risotto liquid cannot.

Mushrooms need high heat, enough space, and to be left alone. Crowding them or using low heat means they steam and go grey and sad and release all their water into a soup situation. Hot pan, space, no movement. Golden mushrooms are the goal.
3

Build the Risotto Base

In the same wide pan, melt 40g butter over medium heat. Add the shallots and thyme and cook for 8 minutes until deeply soft. Add garlic and rehydrated porcini and cook 2 more minutes. Add the rice and stir for 2 minutes until the grains are coated in butter and translucent at the edges. Pour in the wine and stir until fully absorbed.

4

Add Stock Gradually — 18 to 20 Minutes

Add the warm stock one ladle at a time, stirring gently between each addition and waiting until each ladle is absorbed before adding the next. This takes 18–20 minutes total. The rice should be al dente — tender but with a slight bite at the centre. Remove from heat. Stir in the remaining 40g cold butter and the Parmesan vigorously — this is the mantecatura and it creates the creamy, glossy texture. Fold in three-quarters of the sautéed mushrooms. Season.

The mantecatura (the butter-and-cheese finish off the heat) is what makes risotto creamy. If you do it on the heat, the butter splits and you get a greasy, separated dish. Off the heat, cold butter, vigorous stirring. That is the correct sequence.
5

Plate and Finish

Serve immediately into warm bowls. Top with the reserved sautéed mushrooms. Add a drizzle of truffle oil if using. Finish with extra Parmesan and fresh thyme leaves. Risotto waits for no one — serve the moment it's ready.

Deeply Mushroom. Properly Creamy. The One. 🍄

You soaked the porcini and used the liquid. You sautéed the fresh mushrooms at high heat. You kept the stock warm and added it gradually. You finished off the heat with cold butter and Parmesan. This is mushroom risotto done the correct way and it is one of the best vegetarian main courses in existence.
