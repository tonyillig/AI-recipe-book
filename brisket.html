01
The Night Before
The Meat & The Rub
⏱ 20 min prep · overnight rest in fridge
What You Need
6350gwhole packer brisket (14 lbs), USDA Choice or Primepoint + flat attached — don't separate
75gcoarse kosher saltDiamond Crystal; Morton is saltier — use less
75gcoarse ground black pepper (16-mesh)coarse, not fine — this is the bark
1 cupbeef tallow (reserved from trim or purchased)for spritzing and wrapping
1
Trim the Brisket

Lay the brisket fat-side up. Using a sharp boning knife, trim the hard fat cap down to ¼ inch (6mm). Remove any hard "pockets" of fat that won't render — they'll just be waxy in the finished brisket. Flip over and remove the silver skin and any excess membrane from the flat. A well-trimmed brisket cooks more evenly and develops better bark.

Save all the fat you trim. Render it down in a saucepan over low heat for 1–2 hours, strain, and you've got homemade beef tallow — the best thing you can put on a brisket wrap, a cast iron steak, or roasted potatoes.
2
Season — Salt & Pepper, That's It

Mix the kosher salt and coarse black pepper in equal parts. Apply a generous, even coat on all sides of the brisket — fat cap, flat, point, and edges. Don't be shy. This is the entire rub. Aaron Franklin runs one of the most celebrated BBQ programs in Texas history on salt and pepper alone. Trust the process. Let the seasoned brisket rest uncovered in the fridge overnight (or at least 1 hour).

Two ingredients. No paprika. No garlic powder. No sugar. Just salt and pepper. This is either the most confident thing you've ever done in a kitchen or it's the most terrifying. Both can be true at the same time.
Morning of the Cook
02
The Long Game
Smoking the Brisket
⏱ 14–18 hours on the Traeger
Gear & Extras
1 bagpost oak pelletsthe classic Texas choice; use hickory as backup only
1 rollpink (unwaxed) butcher paperNOT foil — foil steams and kills the bark
1spray bottle with water or tallow (melted)
250°FSmoker Temp121°C. Set and maintain throughout.
165°FWrap Temp74°C internal. Wrap in butcher paper.
203°FPull Temp95°C. Probe slides in like butter.
1–2hRest TimeIn a cooler, still wrapped. Non-negotiable.
1
Preheat the Traeger to 250°F / 121°C

Fire up the Traeger with post oak pellets. Preheat to 250°F (121°C) with the lid closed for 15 minutes. Place a drip tray below the grates. Remove the brisket from the fridge 30–45 minutes before it goes on — you're not trying to bring it to room temp, just taking the fridge chill off slightly.

2
Place Fat-Side Up — Then Leave It Alone

Place the brisket fat-side up on the grill grates. Insert your probe thermometer into the thickest part of the flat. Close the lid. Do not open it for 3 hours. Every time you open the lid you dump smoke and heat. The Traeger maintains the temperature — your only job is to let it work.

Some pitmasters prefer fat-side down so the fat "baste" is closer to the heat source. On a Traeger where heat comes from below, fat-side down protects the meat. Try both and see what you prefer — there's genuine debate here.
3
Spritz Every Hour After Hour 3

Starting at hour 3, open the lid quickly and spritz all sides of the brisket with water or melted tallow. Close immediately. Repeat every hour. Spritzing keeps the surface moist so the smoke keeps sticking, prevents over-darkening of the bark, and slows the cook slightly to give collagen more time to break down.

You'll be checking on it every hour for the next 8–10 hours. This is not a recipe. This is a commitment. You planned your whole Saturday around a piece of meat and you have zero regrets.
4
The Stall — Don't Panic

Around 150–165°F internal, the brisket will stop rising in temperature for what may feel like hours. This is called the stall, caused by evaporative cooling as moisture escapes the meat. It is completely normal. Do not raise the temperature. Do not wrap early. Sit with the stall. When you start seeing a dark mahogany bark forming and the temp slowly creeping again — that's when you wrap.

If you panic and crank the heat during the stall you'll tighten the muscle fibers, dry out the flat, and ruin 10 hours of work. The stall is not a problem. The stall is the process.
5
Wrap at 165°F in Pink Butcher Paper

When the internal temp hits 165°F (74°C) and you have a deep, dark, mahogany bark, pull the brisket and wrap tightly in two layers of pink (unwaxed) butcher paper. Add a tablespoon or two of tallow inside the wrap before sealing. Return to the Traeger. The paper lets steam escape (maintaining bark) while retaining enough heat to push through to probe-tender — unlike foil, which steams and ruins the texture.

Wrap tight enough that there's no air pockets but not so tight that you're compressing the brisket. Think: snug, not strangled.
6
Pull at 203°F — or When the Probe Slides Like Butter

Continue cooking until the internal temperature reaches 203°F (95°C). But here's the real test: insert your probe into the thickest part of the flat. It should slide in with almost zero resistance — like pushing into warm butter. If you feel any resistance, keep going. Temperature is a guideline; probe feel is the truth. Most whole packers hit this between 14 and 18 hours total.

Almost There
03
The Most Important Step
The Rest
⏱ 1–2 hours minimum — do not skip
1
Rest in a Cooler for 1–2 Hours

Pull the brisket and do not unwrap it. Place it still wrapped in butcher paper inside a cooler. Layer old towels around and on top of it. Close the cooler. Leave it for at minimum 1 hour, ideally 2. The rest redistributes juices throughout the meat and allows the collagen — which has broken down into gelatin over 14+ hours — to fully set. Skipping the rest means all those juices run out when you slice and your brisket is dry. The rest is not optional.

You waited 14 hours. You can wait one more. The brisket will still be hot after 2 hours in the cooler — this is food science, not vibes. The cooler is basically a budget sous vide. Respect it.
2
Slice Against the Grain — Both Directions

Unwrap the brisket and pour any pooled juices over the top. The point and flat have grain running in different directions. Separate them at the seam of fat between them. Slice the flat against the grain at about pencil-width (¼ inch / 6mm). Slice the point — fattier, more marbled — into thicker cubes or slices for burnt ends. Slicing with the grain gives you tough, stringy meat. Slicing against it gives you melt-in-your-mouth brisket.

The burnt ends from the point are optional but should be considered mandatory. Cube the point into 1-inch pieces, toss in a little tallow and a pinch more rub, return to 275°F for 45–60 minutes until caramelized. Serve separately. People will propose marriage.

14 Hours Well Spent. 🔥

You committed your entire day to this. You trimmed, you rubbed, you sat with the stall, you wrapped in butcher paper, you rested it. This is real BBQ — no shortcuts, no gimmicks, just smoke and time and patience. Set a slice on a piece of white bread, add a pickled jalapeño, eat it standing at the counter. That's the tradition. That's earned.
